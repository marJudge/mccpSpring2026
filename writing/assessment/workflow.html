Workflow Guide

How to Analyze Model Papers and Answer "What Counts as Good Writing?"

A Step-by-Step Process for Research Students

Overview

This workflow guides you through a systematic process to: (1) understand what counts as good writing in your research community, (2) develop a plan to analyze model papers, (3) conduct the analysis focusing on Task Achievement, and (4) generate a professional HTML report. The goal is to help you identify effective writing strategies that you can apply to your own academic writing assignment.

1Reflect on "What Counts as Good Writing"

File to work with: PhDagentSpring2026/writing/assessment/What_counts_as_good_writing_STUDENT.md

What to do:

  • Open the file and read through the structured reflection questions
  • Answer the questions based on your personal experience and research field:
    • What are the expectations for good writing in your research community?
    • What do your target journals/conferences value most?
    • What makes a paper "excellent" in your discipline?
    • Identify 2-3 model papers from your target journals/conferences
  • Consider multiple sources:
    • Reviewers' comments on your previous submissions
    • Feedback from supervisors or peers
    • Model papers published in your target venues
    • Assessment rubrics from this course
  • Update the file with your reflections and save it

Checklist for Step 1:

I have identified my target journals/conferences
I have selected 2-3 model papers to analyze
I have reflected on what makes writing "excellent" in my field
I have considered feedback from reviewers/supervisors/peers
I have updated the What_counts_as_good_writing_STUDENT.md file

2Review Course Materials and Assessment Rubrics

Files to review: PhDagentSpring2026/Materials/md/Session 1_Handout_TEACHER version_word.md
PhDagentSpring2026/Materials/md/Session 2_Handout_TEACHER Version_word.md
PhDagentSpring2026/writing/assessment/writing_instructions_formatted.md

What to do:

  • Review Session 1 materials to understand:
    • The CARS model (Creating a Research Space) for introductions
    • Rhetorical moves: Establishing Territory, Identifying Niche, Occupying Niche
    • Centrality statements and their linguistic features
    • Sample introductions from different disciplines
  • Review Session 2 materials to understand:
    • Literature review moves and organization
    • Synthesis and critical evaluation strategies
    • Reporting verbs and evaluative language
    • Citation and referencing approaches
  • Study the assessment rubrics in writing_instructions_formatted.md:
    • Task Achievement criteria (Excellent/Satisfactory/Unsatisfactory)
    • What "extremely clear" vs "generally clear" means
    • What "skilful synthesis" vs "limited attempt" means
    • Requirements for citations and referencing
Important: Pay special attention to the Task Achievement criteria, as this is what we'll focus on in the analysis. Note the differences between "Excellent" (9-10), "Satisfactory" (6-8), and "Unsatisfactory" (1-5) levels.

3Develop Your Analysis Plan

Reference file: PhDagentSpring2026/writing/assessment/analyzeModelPaperPlan.md

What to do:

  • Review the example plan in analyzeModelPaperPlan.md to understand the structure
  • Create your own analysis plan (you can name it my_analysis_plan.md) that focuses on 5 aspects:
    1. Aspect 1: Research Background and Centrality Statements
      • How do model papers begin their introductions?
      • What linguistic features characterize effective centrality statements?
      • How do authors balance general background with specific research context?
    2. Aspect 2: Research Gap Identification and Delineation
      • How do papers transition from Move 1 to Move 2?
      • What linguistic markers signal gap identification?
      • What makes a gap "clearly delineated" vs "rather vague"?
    3. Aspect 3: Literature Synthesis and Critical Evaluation
    4. Aspect 4: Research Objectives and Significance
    5. Aspect 5: Source Integration, Citation, and Referencing
  • For each aspect, include:
    • What to analyze
    • Key questions to answer
    • Excerpt references from course materials
    • Actionable insights to extract
  • Customize the plan based on your discipline and the model papers you selected

Example Plan Structure:

### Aspect 1: Research Background and Centrality Statements **What to Analyze:** - How authors establish the general territory - Use of centrality statements - Strategies for presenting background **Key Questions:** 1. How do model papers begin their introductions? 2. What linguistic features characterize effective centrality statements? ... **Excerpt Reference (from Session 1):** > "Organic electronics have drawn enormous attention..."

4Select Model Papers

Where to find papers: PhDagentSpring2026/literature/[YourFolderName]/

What to do:

  • Select 2-3 model papers from your target journals/conferences
  • Ensure papers represent "excellent" examples of Task Achievement:
    • Papers that have received positive reviews
    • Frequently cited papers in your field
    • Papers that demonstrate clear research background, gap identification, and synthesis
  • Save papers in your literature folder (create a folder with your name or identifier if needed)
  • Note the file paths for reference in your analysis
Note: You can start by analyzing just 2 papers focusing on Aspects 1 and 2 (as shown in the example), then scale up to analyze more papers and more aspects later. The framework is designed to be scalable.

5Analyze Papers Following Your Plan

Reference example: PhDagentSpring2026/literature/GFY*/task_achievement_analysis.html

What to do:

  • For each paper, systematically annotate:
    • Introduction section: Identify rhetorical moves (CARS model), mark centrality statements, gap identification, objective statements
    • Literature Review section: Identify thematic organization, synthesis patterns, critical evaluation markers, citation patterns
    • Language features: Highlight reporting verbs, evaluative language, hedging, comparative language, signposting
  • Extract excerpts that demonstrate excellent Task Achievement for each aspect
  • Compare patterns across papers to identify common strategies
  • Note discipline-specific conventions and variations
  • Document your findings with specific excerpts and explanations

Example Analysis Structure:

### Paper 1: [Title] #### Aspect 1: Research Background **Excerpt:** "Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have demonstrated remarkable effectiveness..." **Analysis:** - Uses evaluative language: "remarkable effectiveness" - Present perfect tense: "have demonstrated" - Multiple citations showing field knowledge - Clear progression from general to specific

6Generate HTML Analysis Report

Example output: PhDagentSpring2026/literature/GFY*/task_achievement_analysis.html

What to do:

  • Create an HTML file (name it task_achievement_analysis.html or similar)
  • Structure your report to include:
    • Header with paper information (titles, authors, venues)
    • For each aspect: side-by-side comparison of papers
    • Excerpts with highlighting and analysis
    • Actionable insights section
    • Comparison tables (if analyzing multiple aspects)
  • Make it visually appealing:
    • Use CSS for styling (colors, fonts, layout)
    • Color-code different papers
    • Use boxes and borders to highlight important sections
    • Ensure responsive design (works on different screen sizes)
  • Include actionable insights:
    • Pattern identification (common structures, phrases)
    • Quality indicators (what distinguishes "excellent" from "satisfactory")
    • Practical strategies students can apply
    • Language resources (useful phrases, reporting verbs)
  • Save the HTML file in your literature folder: PhDagentSpring2026/literature/[YourFolderName]/task_achievement_analysis.html

HTML Report Structure Example:

<!DOCTYPE html> <html> <head> <title>Task Achievement Analysis</title> <style> /* Add CSS styling here */ </style> </head> <body> <div class="container"> <header> <h1>Task Achievement Analysis</h1> </header> <div class="aspect-section"> <h2>Aspect 1: Research Background</h2> <div class="comparison-grid"> <div class="paper-analysis"> <h3>Paper 1</h3> <div class="excerpt">...</div> <div class="analysis">...</div> </div> <div class="paper-analysis"> <h3>Paper 2</h3> ... </div> </div> </div> </div> </body> </html>
Tip: You can view the example HTML file at PhDagentSpring2026/literature/GFY*/task_achievement_analysis.html to see a complete working example with styling and structure.

7Submit Your Work

What to do:

  • Ensure your HTML file is saved in the correct location: PhDagentSpring2026/literature/[YourFolderName]/task_achievement_analysis.html
  • Commit and push to GitHub (if using version control):
    • git add literature/[YourFolderName]/task_achievement_analysis.html
    • git commit -m "Add task achievement analysis"
    • git push
  • In your Moodle post, reply with:
    • The file path to your HTML file
    • A brief description of what you analyzed
    • Key insights you discovered

Example Moodle Post:

I have completed my analysis of two model papers focusing on Task Achievement (Aspects 1 and 2). File path: PhDagentSpring2026/literature/[MyName]/task_achievement_analysis.html Key findings: - Both papers use evaluative language in centrality statements - Gap identification strategies differ: one uses enumeration, the other uses concrete examples - Both demonstrate high citation density in Move 1 The analysis has helped me understand what "extremely clear" research background and "clearly delineated" gaps look like in practice.
Important: Make sure your file path follows this format: PhDagentSpring2026/literature/[YourFolderName]/task_achievement_analysis.html Replace [YourFolderName] with your actual folder name (e.g., your name or identifier).

Summary: The Complete Workflow

  1. Reflect on what counts as good writing in your field (What_counts_as_good_writing_STUDENT.md)
  2. Review course materials and rubrics (Session 1 & 2, writing_instructions_formatted.md)
  3. Plan your analysis approach (create your own analysis plan based on analyzeModelPaperPlan.md)
  4. Select 2-3 model papers from your target journals/conferences
  5. Analyze papers systematically following your plan (focus on Task Achievement aspects)
  6. Generate an HTML report with excerpts, analysis, and actionable insights
  7. Submit the file path in Moodle: PhDagentSpring2026/literature/[YourFolderName]/task_achievement_analysis.html
